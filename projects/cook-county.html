Property Valuation & Bias Detection - Cook County, Illinois

View Notebook GitHub Repo Open Paper Viewer

Abstract

This study examines the determinants of 2013 to 2019 residential property sale prices in Cook County, Illinois using a three-stage linear modeling approach. Beginning with a simple univariate model based on bedroom count, I iteratively introduce building size and then a comprehensive set of engineered features including log-transformed area measures, tax assessor estimates, fireplace counts, repair condition scores, central air conditioning status, and categorical encodings of room counts.

My final model trained on 204,792 property transactions achieves a root-mean-square error (RMSE) of 0.3716 in predicting log sale price, representing a substantial 57% improvement over the baseline. I also find that lower-priced homes are systematically overestimated, illustrating a regressive bias that can exacerbate tax burdens on low-income residents.

1. Introduction

Accurate prediction of residential sale prices is crucial for market transparency, equitable property taxation, and urban planning. This project documents a reproducible modeling workflow and uses residual analysis to surface fairness concerns, especially in lower-value housing segments.

2. Data Description

The primary dataset contains 204,792 records and 62 features, including physical housing attributes, material characteristics, condition assessments, and tax assessor estimates for land and building values.

3. Data Cleaning & Exploratory Analysis

  1. Outlier removal: Excluded properties with sale prices below $499 to eliminate data-entry errors and symbolic transactions.
  2. Missing values: Removed or imputed missing entries for critical predictors.
  3. Distributions: Sale prices were right-skewed, so a log transformation was used.
  4. Correlation analysis: Building square footage and assessor estimates showed strong positive correlations with sale price.
  5. Feature relationships: Residual analysis motivated log transforms and expanded predictors.

4. Modeling Framework

  1. Baseline model: Bedrooms only.
  2. Intermediate model: Bedrooms plus log building square footage.
  3. Final model: 13 engineered predictors with linear regression and RMSE evaluation.

5. Results & Evaluation

Model Engineered Feature(s) RMSE (multiplier error)
Model 1 Bedrooms 0.8674 (~x2.38 error)
Model 2 Bedrooms + log area 0.8059 (~x2.24 error)
Model 3 13 predictors 0.3716 (~x1.45 error)

6. Discussion & Next Steps

  1. Interpretability & equity: The model remains interpretable, but regressive bias leads to disproportionate tax burdens on lower-income owners.
  2. Systemic context: Appeal access, neighborhood demographics, and historical segregation shape how these errors land in practice.
  3. Residual analysis: The lowest-price homes are systematically overestimated.
  4. Future work: Test spatial or fairness-aware models and build stakeholder-facing reporting tools.

7. Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates that a reproducible hedonic modeling workflow can sharply reduce error while still exposing harmful bias patterns. Predictive systems should be evaluated not only for technical accuracy but also for whether their mistakes disproportionately affect vulnerable communities.
